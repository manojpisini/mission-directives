# Evidence Lanes

Every task uses one lane. The lane controls how facts, interpretation, and invention may appear.

## Factual

Use for research, academic work, analysis, business cases, reports, policies, dashboards, financial models, and factual visuals.

- Material claims require traceable evidence.
- Current or decision-critical claims require current sources.
- Calculations expose inputs, units, and assumptions.
- Unknowns, disagreement, missingness, and limitations remain visible.
- No invented citations, quotes, data, experiments, approvals, or results.

## Hybrid

Use for blogs, scripts, brand, campaigns, pitches, presentations, events, and explanatory design that combines facts with interpretation or creative craft.

- Sourced facts remain traceable.
- Interpretation and creative choices are labeled or structurally distinct.
- Narrative, visual, and rhetorical devices may improve communication but may not alter evidence.
- Claims, testimonials, offers, comparisons, and numbers remain verifiable.

## Imaginative

Use for fiction, poetry, prose, speculative concepts, and other work whose value is primarily artistic.

- Optimize for originality, internal coherence, craft, and intended effect.
- Do not fabricate citations or imply factual validation.
- Avoid imitation of living creators and avoid generic, clichéd, repetitive output.

## Lane escalation

A prompt may move from imaginative to hybrid or factual when it introduces real people, history, science, medicine, law, finance, brands, statistics, or public claims. The stricter lane governs those elements.
